# PRD Quality Review — unoriginally-deterministic

## Overall verdict
This is a well-earned PRD for its actual shape: a solo, dogfood-first hobby tool that happens to ship publicly. Six of seven dimensions hold up strong — the thesis is sharp, deliberate scope calls (COBOL-first, Claude-Code-only reliability gate, closed contributions, no adoption tracking) are stated as decisions with rationale rather than smoothed over, and IDs/glossary/cross-refs are clean enough for architecture and epics to source-extract from directly. The one real risk sits in Done-ness clarity: FR-2, which the PRD itself calls "the single most important piece of design work in the project," leans on unbounded language ("small enough," "short enough") at the exact place downstream work needs a testable bar.

## Decision-readiness — strong
Decisions are stated as decisions, not buried as considerations: COBOL-first is named and justified ("chosen because it's the maintainer's own most pressing comprehension problem, not as a general language-coverage commitment," §4.1), tool ownership lives in the user's repo "so it keeps working even if the toolkit project stalls" (§4.4), and community contributions are explicitly deferred with the gap named rather than hidden ("no defined trigger yet for when that opens," §6.2). Trade-offs are named with what's given up, not just what was chosen — e.g. §4.1's Out of Scope line on commercial COBOL dialects: "available only as a user-supplied plug-in via an extension point, never bundled in core." Open Questions (§8) are genuinely open rather than rhetorical: OQ-1 is a real go/no-go blocker, OQ-2 admits the core design isn't done, OQ-4 has no answer at all yet.

### Findings
- **low** OQ-5 supplies most of its own answer (§8) — The question "what counts as a striking enough dogfood outcome" is followed immediately by a detailed "Leading candidate" paragraph (self-documentation, with a Glamorous Toolkit comparison) that reads more like a decision-in-waiting than an open question. It is honestly caveated ("Not yet committed to as THE answer"), so this is not a dodge, but it sits close to the rubric's "rhetorical question with an answer in the next sentence" pattern. *Fix:* either commit to it as the SM-3 answer and move the reasoning to the Assumptions Index, or trim OQ-5 back to the bare question and move the candidate-answer reasoning to a working note outside the PRD.

## Substance over theater — strong
No persona theater (a single named user, "the maintainer," drives every JTBD and UJ — no persona padding). No innovation theater — the differentiation claim ("LLMs are coherence engines, not truth engines... trust is enforced structurally, not by convention," §1) is specific and load-bearing, not a template section. No NFR theater — rather than papering over performance/security with boilerplate adjectives, the PRD explicitly defers cross-cutting NFRs to the architecture phase and says so plainly (§9: "explicitly deferred to the architecture phase per maintainer direction — not resolved in this PRD"), which is honest scoping rather than theater. The Vision statement (§1) is specific to this exact product and could not swap into another PRD unchanged.

## Strategic coherence — strong
The thesis is explicit and singular: non-determinism is spent once at tool-build time (where a human can review it), then the tool runs deterministically forever (§1). Feature ordering follows the thesis, not ease-of-build: extraction (§4.1) feeds the extension contract's enforced provenance (§4.2, explicitly flagged as "the core reliability mechanism"), which feeds starter views (§4.3), which feeds tool ownership independent of the toolkit's survival (§4.4) — each stage protects a different piece of the "runs forever, for free" claim. Success Metrics validate the thesis rather than measuring activity: SM-1 is a comprehension-speed outcome, SM-2 is a direct reliability-of-the-mechanism gate, and both counter-metrics (SM-C1, SM-C2) explicitly forbid trading provenance correctness or comprehension substance for adoption breadth or view/language count — a textbook case of naming the counter-metric rather than letting every SM read as universally good. MVP scope reads as coherent problem-solving scope, not a backlog with headings.

## Done-ness clarity — adequate
Most FRs carry genuinely testable consequences: FR-3's provenance mechanism ("a call omitting it does not emit anything") is mechanically verifiable, and FR-4/FR-5's consequences ("no setup, no account, no AI/tokens spent," "no telemetry or network call") are binary and checkable. But FR-2 — the FR under the feature the PRD itself calls the single most important design surface in the project (§4.2) — states its key consequences in unbounded, subjective terms: "small enough that its correct usage is the path of least resistance," and "short enough to be read end-to-end." Neither gives an engineer or a future story-writer a bar to build or test against; both are closer to the rubric's flagged pattern ("reasonable performance," "user-friendly") than to a testable condition. This matters more here than it would elsewhere in the PRD because FR-2 is explicitly the load-bearing FR the reliability gate (OQ-1/SM-2) exists to validate.

### Findings
- **medium** FR-2's consequences lack any bound (§4.2, FR-2) — "The primitive API surface is small enough..." and "A molded tool's own source is short enough..." give no threshold (line count, call count, etc.), for the FR the PRD calls its most important design work. *Fix:* attach a provisional numeric bound (e.g. "molded tool source ≤ ~150 LOC," "primitive surface ≤ N calls for a starter view"), even a rough one to be corrected by the OQ-1 reliability-gate results, so architecture/stories have something to test against.
- **low** FR-1's third consequence is a design-intent statement, not a test (§4.1, FR-1) — "The extraction pipeline is not hardcoded to COBOL-only assumptions that would block adding a second language later" is not independently checkable as written. *Fix:* rephrase as a concrete architectural check (e.g. "the extraction entry point accepts a grammar/language identifier parameter") or move it out of "Consequences (testable)" into a design-intent note.

## Scope honesty — strong
Non-Goals (§5) does real work — eight explicit non-goals, each with its own one-line rationale, not a boilerplate list. The tension the task brief called out — public open-source launch stakes vs. dogfood-only success criteria — is not left for the reader to infer; the PRD names it itself in §7's framing note: "Personal/dogfood-scale per the maintainer's explicit framing — depth here is closer to a hobby PRD's single-sentence bar than a launch PRD's quantitative breakdown, even though the toolkit ships publicly." Counter-metric SM-C1 does the same work for adoption tracking specifically. De-scoping is proposed honestly rather than silently: §6.2's rollout plan admits "no defined trigger yet" for opening community contributions, rather than implying a plan that doesn't exist. Open-items density (5 OQs, 4 assumptions, 0 NOTE FOR PM) is proportionate to a solo/hobby PRD and each item reads as a real gap, not padding.

### Findings
- **low** No `[NOTE FOR PM]` callouts anywhere in the document — Given the deferred/unresolved items that exist (e.g. OQ-4's contribution trigger, OQ-5's showcase bar), a PM-facing PRD would typically flag these inline. Here the Open Questions section absorbs that function instead, and since the maintainer is both PM and sole reader, this is likely immaterial. *Fix:* none needed unless this PRD is later handed to a collaborator who isn't the maintainer, at which point the deferred items in §8 should get explicit `[NOTE FOR PM]` treatment.

## Downstream usability — strong
This PRD is chain-top (§0: "written for... whoever picks up architecture, epics, and implementation next"), so this dimension matters at full weight, not the reduced weight given to standalone PRDs. FR/UJ/SM/OQ IDs are contiguous and unique (FR-1…FR-5, UJ-1…UJ-3, SM-1…SM-3 plus SM-C1/C2, OQ-1…OQ-5) with no gaps or duplicates. Cross-references resolve cleanly on inspection: each Feature's "Realizes UJ-n" and each SM's "Validates FR-n" point to IDs that exist and match the described work (e.g. SM-2 "Validates FR-2, FR-3" correctly ties the reliability gate to the extension contract and provenance FRs). The Glossary (§3) is used consistently across FRs — "molded tool," "provenance," "starter view," and "reliability gate" all appear in FR/SM text exactly as defined. All three UJs carry a named protagonist ("the maintainer") throughout, satisfying the rubric's no-floating-UJ check.

### Findings
- **low** "Tool-builder" is used but not glossary-defined (§4.2, FR-2) — §4.2 explicitly widens this role beyond the maintainer ("Claude Code or a human, working directly in the engineer's own repo"), so it isn't a synonym for "the maintainer" and deserves its own entry alongside "molded tool" in §3. *Fix:* add a one-line Glossary definition for "tool-builder" (AI assistant or human author of a molded tool).

## Shape fit — strong
The PRD is unusually self-aware about its own shape-fit calibration rather than leaving the reader to guess: §2.3 flags "Solo/hobby-scoped per the template's lighter dial — single-sentence form," and §7 flags the personal/dogfood-scale bar directly. This matches the rubric's guidance ("Hobby / solo → rigor light, substance bar still applies") almost exactly, and the PRD names its own choice instead of the reviewer having to infer it — that self-naming is itself a mark of a PRD that understands its own genre. UJs are appropriately light (three single-sentence UJs, one protagonist) rather than padded to look thorough, and there's no enterprise-shaped NFR or persona bloat despite the "ships publicly" framing. The COBOL-first, Claude-Code-only, and closed-contributions choices — the three decisions the task brief flagged as deliberate — are each stated plainly with reasoning (§4.1, §5, §6.2) rather than defended defensively or hidden.

## Mechanical notes
- **Unresolved template placeholder**: line 9, "*Working title — confirm.*" is still present under the title — trivial, but should be resolved (or explicitly kept) before this PRD is treated as final.
- **Assumptions Index roundtrip gap**: two of the four §9 entries have no corresponding inline `[ASSUMPTION]` tag anywhere in the document body — "Cross-cutting NFRs (performance budgets, supported VS Code versions/OS) are explicitly deferred to the architecture phase" and "Toolkit-level... versioning/deprecation policy is undefined at v1" both appear only in §9, not flagged inline where they'd apply (e.g. no NFR section to tag, and no versioning-policy FR to tag against). This isn't necessarily wrong — there may be no natural inline anchor for cross-cutting items — but it's a partial roundtrip break worth knowing about.
- **Glossary gap**: see Downstream usability finding above ("tool-builder" used, not defined).
- **ID continuity**: clean across FR/UJ/SM/OQ — no gaps, no duplicates.
- **Cross-references**: all "Realizes UJ-n" / "Validates FR-n" references resolve to existing IDs correctly.
- **Required sections for the agreed stakes**: all present (Vision, Target User, Glossary, Features/FRs, Non-Goals, MVP Scope, Success Metrics, Open Questions, Assumptions Index) — nothing missing for a solo/hobby-shaped, publicly-shipped toolkit PRD.
